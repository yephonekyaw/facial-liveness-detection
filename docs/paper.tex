\begin{abstract}
Face anti-spoofing (PAD) models trained on a single benchmark routinely
achieve near-zero error within their own test set yet fail badly when
transferred to a different attack family or capture device. We argue
that the dominant cost in closing this gap is not architectural but
\emph{logistical}: each public benchmark ships in a different format,
schema, and split convention. We present a unified RGB-only preprocessing
pipeline that collapses two structurally different datasets ---
Replay-Attack (2D photo/video) and 3DMAD (3D mask) --- into a single
CSV manifest of $77{,}296$ identically-shaped $256{\times}256$ face
crops, and train AttackNet v2.2, a compact $8{.}4$M-parameter residual
CNN, from scratch on their union. Within-dataset test ACER is below
$2\%$ on both benchmarks. Cross-dataset evaluation reveals a sharp
asymmetry: Replay $\rightarrow$ 3DMAD transfers at $2{.}8\%$ frame ACER
(HTER $8{.}8\%$ after devel calibration), whereas 3DMAD $\rightarrow$
Replay collapses to $38{.}8\%$ (HTER $22{.}7\%$) --- evidence that
single-dataset models learn protocol-specific shortcuts rather than a
general notion of liveness. A held-out probe on CSMAD (silicone mask
attacks, Intel RealSense SR300) shows that combined training does not
automatically inherit the better of the two single-dataset transfers:
the combined and replay-only models reach HTER $\approx 0{.}46$--$0{.}48$
(AUC $\approx 0{.}63$), while the 3DMAD-only model transfers substantially
better (HTER $0{.}23$, AUC $0{.}85$). For silicone-mask threats
specifically, the 3DMAD-conditioned prior is the more useful one.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

A face presentation attack detector (PAD) sees the same input as a face
verification system --- a photograph of a face --- and must decide
whether the photograph depicts a live human or a presentation
artefact: a printed photograph, a video replayed on a phone screen,
or a silicone mask. The literature reports two seemingly inconsistent
facts: modern CNN-based detectors saturate the standard public
benchmarks (Replay-Attack \cite{anjos2011replay}, 3DMAD
\cite{erdogmus20143dmad}, CSMAD \cite{bhattacharjee2018csmad})
at sub-$1\%$ error; yet PAD models that are deployed without
retraining on the target distribution fail far more often than these
numbers would suggest. The cross-dataset gap is now well-documented
and is a direct consequence of training a classifier on a single,
narrow capture protocol.

This paper does not propose a new architecture. Instead we make a
deliberately mundane methodological argument: the most actionable lever
for closing the cross-dataset gap, before any architectural change,
is to \emph{merge the public benchmarks at the data layer} and train a
single model on their union. The benefit per unit of effort is large,
because (i) the public benchmarks have systematically different
weaknesses and (ii) a uniform manifest is a one-time engineering cost
that all subsequent experiments amortise.

Concretely, our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=2pt]
\item A unified RGB preprocessing pipeline that ingests Replay-Attack
   (1{,}200 \texttt{.mov} videos) and 3DMAD (255 HDF5 frame stacks),
   detects faces with a single shared detector (RetinaFace via
   \texttt{insightface}), and emits a single manifest of
   $77{,}296$ uniformly-cropped face JPEGs.

\item An implementation of AttackNet v2.2, a compact two-block residual
   CNN with $8.4$M parameters, trained from scratch with mixed-precision
   on a single GTX 1070 (8\,GB VRAM). The model is small by design ---
   liveness is largely a texture problem, and ImageNet-pretrained
   backbones are biased \emph{against} the texture cues PAD needs.

\item A complete experimental matrix --- combined training,
   single-dataset baselines, $5$-fold subject-disjoint cross-validation,
   and zero-shot cross-dataset evaluation --- with both fixed-threshold
   and devel-calibrated HTER reporting.

\item A devel-calibrated out-of-distribution probe on CSMAD (silicone
   mask attacks, Intel RealSense SR300), enabled by a forward-compatible
   manifest schema that required no changes to the training code. The
   probe shows that the combined and replay-only models both struggle on
   CSMAD (HTER ${\sim}0{.}46$--$0{.}48$, AUC ${\sim}0{.}63$), whereas
   the 3DMAD-only model transfers far better (HTER $0{.}23$, AUC
   $0{.}85$). Mask-shape priors learned from one rigid-mask family carry
   over to silicone masks across a sensor change in a way that screen-
   and print-attack priors do not.
\end{enumerate}

We make no architectural claim of novelty for AttackNet v2.2: the
network is a pragmatic choice for a project sized to a single consumer
GPU. Our contribution is the pipeline and the empirical finding that a
near-balanced union of two structurally different benchmarks is enough
to recover combined-domain accuracy without sacrificing single-domain
performance.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

The two training datasets span the two canonical attack families.
Replay-Attack \cite{anjos2011replay} contains $1{,}300$ Motion-JPEG
videos from $50$ clients, covering print, mobile, high-definition
and video replay attacks. 3DMAD \cite{erdogmus20143dmad} contains
$255$ HDF5 clips at $640{\times}480$ from a Microsoft Kinect, covering
real access and 3D-printed mask attacks for $17$ subjects. CSMAD
\cite{bhattacharjee2018csmad} extends this into the silicone-mask
regime with multi-modal captures from a RealSense SR300 and a Seek
thermal camera; we use only its RGB channel. The architectural
ingredients we rely on are standard --- residual additive shortcuts
\cite{he2016resnet}, batch normalisation \cite{ioffe2015batchnorm},
leaky rectifiers \cite{maas2013leaky}, Kaiming initialisation
\cite{he2015kaiming}, and Adam \cite{kingma2014adam}. The cross-dataset
gap in PAD is well documented; reporting follows ISO/IEC~30107-3
\cite{iso301073}: APCER (attacks classified bonafide), BPCER (bonafide
classified attack), and ACER as their mean.

\section{Data Unification}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{Manifest Design}

Replay-Attack and 3DMAD share no common I/O interface: one delivers
Motion-JPEG video containers, the other delivers HDF5 frame stacks
with per-frame depth maps and eye-coordinate annotations. A training
loop that handles both natively must branch on dataset identity
throughout, which makes adding a third dataset expensive and error-prone.
We therefore collapse all datasets into a single offline-computed
artefact before any learning code runs:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item A flat directory of identically-shaped $256{\times}256$ RGB JPEG
   face crops, organised as \texttt{data/processed/<dataset>/}.
\item A single CSV manifest in which each row describes one crop and
   carries all metadata required by training, evaluation, and analysis.
\end{enumerate}

Table~\ref{tab:schema} lists the manifest schema. \texttt{subject\_id}
is globally namespaced so subject-disjoint splitting requires no
per-dataset logic, and the training loop filters on \texttt{split} and
\texttt{label} only --- making it dataset-agnostic by construction.

We use RetinaFace as a single shared detector across all three datasets,
specifically the lightweight \texttt{buffalo\_sc} bundle from
\texttt{insightface} (${\sim}14$\,MB \texttt{det\_500m.onnx}), executed
via \texttt{onnxruntime-gpu} for compatibility with Pascal-class hardware.
Per-video bounding-box detections are cached to disk so that re-running
the extractor incurs no additional detection cost.

For each detected face we take the highest-confidence box, expand it
by a factor of $1{+}0.3 = 1.3$ to capture peripheral context (hair,
jawline, screen bezel), square-pad to the longer side, clamp to the
image bounds, and resize to $256{\times}256$ with Lanczos
interpolation. Lanczos preserves high-frequency texture, which is the
signal that a screen replay's moir\'e or a printed page's grain
actually carries; bilinear or area resampling smooths these cues
out. We save as JPEG quality $95$ ($\sim$20\,KB per crop), striking a
balance between disk footprint and texture preservation.

\subsection{Splits, Frame Sampling, and Modality}

We sample every fifth frame ($\text{stride}=5$) from each source video.
Adjacent frames in the source recordings are highly redundant; retaining
all frames increases training time by approximately $3{\times}$ with no
measurable benefit to validation accuracy. The resulting count is
$77{,}296$ crops across the two training datasets
(Table~\ref{tab:class-distribution}); CSMAD contributes a further
$18{,}763$ crops, partitioned by subject into train/devel/test but
held out from training in every model reported below --- it appears
only as an out-of-distribution test corpus.

For Replay-Attack we follow the dataset's official train/devel/test
partition. 3DMAD does not ship a canonical split, so we use a
deterministic, reproducible subject-level partition: subjects 01--07
$\rightarrow$ train, 08--12 $\rightarrow$ devel, 13--17
$\rightarrow$ test (a 7/5/5 split). For CSMAD we partition the eleven
distinct subjects (A, B, G, H, I in train; C, D, J, K in devel;
E, F, L, M, N in test); attacks are present in every split but only
cover subjects A--F, so each split contains a mix of seen and unseen
identities on the bonafide side. Anything more elaborate
(stratification by gender, age, etc.) requires metadata the datasets
do not publish.

Every crop is RGB only. We deliberately discard 3DMAD's depth channel
and CSMAD's IR, depth, and thermal channels. The argument is
threefold: (i) the manifest schema stays modality-uniform, so a single
network architecture covers all three datasets; (ii) we are
benchmarking the harder problem (RGB-only PAD), since modern phones
do not all ship depth or thermal sensors; and (iii) a multi-modal
architecture would inflate the model's parameter count beyond what the
8\,GB GTX 1070 budget can fit alongside Optuna trials.

\textbf{Class balance is a happy accident.} The training datasets are
imbalanced in opposite directions (Table~\ref{tab:class-distribution},
Figure~\ref{fig:class-dist}). Replay is $\sim$76\% attack at the frame
level (300 attack vs.\ 60 bonafide source videos per split); 3DMAD is
$\sim$67\% bonafide (sessions 01 and 02 are both real, only session 03
is the mask attack). On their union the two imbalances cancel: the
combined train split is $16{,}200$ bonafide vs.\ $16{,}196$ attack,
near-perfectly balanced without any explicit re-weighting. CSMAD is
itself attack-heavy ($\sim$80\% attack; 9 mask attackers vs.\ 14
bonafide subjects across the original archive), but since CSMAD is
held out from training in every model reported, its imbalance is a
property of the OOD test corpus rather than a constraint on training.
We therefore do \emph{not} use weighted sampling for the combined
protocol; the single-dataset baselines retain the raw imbalance, which
is the regime they would be deployed in anyway.

\textbf{Quality distributions are dataset-distinctive.} On a stratified
sample of $5{,}000$ crops we computed four standard image-quality
metrics (Laplacian variance, RMS contrast, mean brightness, Tenengrad
gradient magnitude). As shown in Figure~\ref{fig:quality}, 3DMAD is
uniformly clean and controlled with the lowest Laplacian and Tenengrad
spread; Replay is broadest along every axis, with brightness pulled
down by the adverse-lighting subset and Tenengrad reaching $\sim$$180$
on the highest-frequency screen attacks. CSMAD occupies a third,
visibly distinct band: its Laplacian sharpness is markedly higher than
3DMAD's (the SR300 colour stream is high-resolution and well-focused),
its contrast distribution sits below both other datasets, and its
brightness is mid-range but tighter than Replay's. A model trained
only on 3DMAD has never encountered Replay's low-brightness,
high-noise regime; a model trained only on Replay has seen only the
high-Tenengrad world of screens and prints; neither has seen anything
resembling CSMAD's high-sharpness, low-contrast SR300 imagery.
Combined training spans the Replay+3DMAD region but not CSMAD's,
which is the proximate cause of the OOD behaviour in
Section~\ref{sec:csmad}.

\textbf{The three datasets occupy disjoint regions in feature space.}
Embedding $\sim$1{,}000 crops per (dataset, label) cell with a frozen
ImageNet ResNet-18 and projecting via PCA and t-SNE shows that bonafide
and attack classes overlap heavily by label, but the three datasets
form visibly disjoint clusters. The relevant texture cues are not
represented in ImageNet features --- a purpose-built CNN trained from
scratch is therefore necessary --- and CSMAD's cluster sits well
outside both training distributions, foreshadowing the OOD behaviour
in Section~\ref{sec:csmad}.


\section{Method: AttackNet v2.2}
\label{sec:method}

AttackNet v2.2 is a deliberately small CNN ($8{.}4$M parameters in our
implementation) compared to ResNet-50 ($\sim$$25$M) or VGG-16
($\sim$$138$M). On a constrained domain (single-modality face crops,
$\sim$$10^5$ training images), wider pretrained backbones overfit to
identity and dataset artefacts rather than learning liveness. A
compact model trained from scratch with the right regularisation
performs better in this regime.

\subsection{Architecture}

The network is two residual convolutional blocks followed by a dense
classification head, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:architecture}.

Three deliberate design choices distinguish v2.2:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,topsep=2pt,itemsep=1pt]
\item \textbf{Skip connections add, not concatenate.} Concatenation
   doubles channels and inflates the next conv. Addition keeps channels
   constant at similar accuracy, following the original
   ResNet \cite{he2016resnet} convention.
\item \textbf{LeakyReLU on the dense head.} The flatten-to-$64$
   bottleneck has $\sim$$8.4$M incoming weights; a vanilla ReLU there
   can permanently zero out useful directions early in training (the
   ``dying ReLU'' problem \cite{maas2013leaky}). The leaky variant
   preserves a small gradient through negative activations.
\item \textbf{BatchNorm everywhere.} After every conv and the dense
   bottleneck. Standardising activations across the batch lets us use
   a slightly larger effective learning rate without divergence.
\end{enumerate}

then MaxPool and Dropout2d. The dense head maps $131{,}072$ flattened features
through a 64-unit bottleneck (LeakyReLU, $\alpha{=}0.2$) to a single logit;
$\sigma(\text{logit})>0.5$ is the attack decision boundary.}
\label{fig:architecture}
\end{figure*}

\subsection{Output head and parameter budget}

We use a single linear layer producing one logit per sample, paired
with \texttt{BCEWithLogitsLoss} at training time --- numerically
equivalent to sigmoid + BCE but more stable. The decision boundary is
$\text{logit} = 0$ (equivalently $\sigma(\text{logit})=0{.}5$).

Of the $8{,}406{,}321$ total parameters, $99{.}8\%$ live in the first
dense layer ($131{,}072 \to 64 = 8{,}388{,}672$ weights). This is
typical for a compact-CNN-plus-wide-MLP-head architecture; the
alternative is widening the conv stack, which we have no evidence is
needed at this dataset scale. Memory at inference and training is
modest: peak VRAM at batch $16$ in fp32 is $682$\,MB, leaving a
$12{\times}$ headroom factor on the $8$\,GB GTX 1070.

\section{Training Protocol}
\label{sec:training}

\subsection{Loss with label smoothing}

We optimise BCE-with-logits against smoothed labels. Given a hard
target $y\in\{0,1\}$ and smoothing $s\!\in\![0,1)$:
\begin{equation}
\tilde y \;=\; y\cdot(1-2s)+s,\qquad
\mathcal{L} \;=\; \text{BCEWithLogits}(\hat y,\,\tilde y).
\end{equation}
We default $s=0.1$, which improves calibration and modestly reduces
overfitting without shifting the decision boundary.

The defaults are listed in Table~\ref{tab:training-defaults}. The
learning rate of $1\!\times\!10^{-6}$ is startlingly small but
defensible: the dense layer dominates the parameter count, and large
LRs blow up its BatchNorm statistics within the first few steps. With
BatchNorm everywhere, internal covariate effects are already mostly
tamed, so the schedule's job is slow refinement rather than fast
descent. Augmentation, applied to the train split only via
Albumentations: rotation $\pm 20^\circ$, horizontal flip ($p\!=\!0{.}5$),
brightness/contrast $\pm 20\%$ ($p\!=\!0{.}5$), Gaussian noise
$\sigma \in [0{.}005,\,0{.}03]$ ($p\!=\!0{.}3$), and motion blur with
kernel up to $5$ ($p\!=\!0{.}3$). We do not apply ImageNet-stat
normalisation --- the network trains from scratch, so plain $[0,1]$
scaling is appropriate.

Mixed precision is enabled by default. On Pascal it halves activation
memory (the conv blocks save $\sim$$250$\,MB at batch $16$), which
matters during Optuna trials when other processes might share the GPU.
If a trial diverges with NaN losses, we disable AMP first with
\texttt{--no-amp} before suspecting other causes.

\subsection{Metrics}

We follow ISO/IEC~30107-3 \cite{iso301073} with the convention
$\text{label}=1\Rightarrow\text{attack}$:
\begin{align}
\text{APCER} &= \tfrac{\#\{\text{attack predicted bonafide}\}}{\#\,\text{attack}}, \\
\text{BPCER} &= \tfrac{\#\{\text{bonafide predicted attack}\}}{\#\,\text{bonafide}}, \\
\text{ACER}  &= \tfrac{1}{2}(\text{APCER}+\text{BPCER}), \\
\text{EER}   &= \text{error at the threshold where APCER}=\text{BPCER}, \\
\text{HTER}  &= \text{ACER at an externally-fixed threshold}.
\end{align}
ACER is the single number we minimise during training (the early-stop
criterion is val-ACER, not val-loss). HTER is the headline
cross-protocol number, and we calibrate its threshold on the target
dataset's devel split before reporting on its test split --- an honest
protocol that does not peek at test labels.

We additionally report \emph{video-level} numbers: per-video
probabilities are obtained by averaging frame probabilities over a
\texttt{source\_video}, then re-thresholded. This reduces frame-wise
noise and is the standard headline reporting style for video-based
liveness benchmarks.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{The asymmetry is the finding.} A model trained on
Replay-Attack's diverse 2D presentations (print, mobile, high-def,
video; 50 subjects; controlled and adverse lighting) carries some of
that diversity into the cross-dataset regime. A model trained on
3DMAD's single attack type ($17$ subjects, one lighting condition,
one camera, one mask family) has a brittle decision surface that does
not transfer. The takeaway is that \emph{training-data diversity
matters more than training-set size}: combined training closes most of
the within-distribution gap at negligible cost ($1{.}85\%$ vs.\ $1{.}09\%$
frame ACER on Replay-test, $0\%$ on 3DMAD-test), but does not help on
a third domain its data does not cover (Section~\ref{sec:csmad}).

\textbf{Limitations.} (i) RGB-only; multi-modal use of 3DMAD's depth
or CSMAD's IR/thermal channels would change the problem. (ii) A single
detector (RetinaFace, \texttt{insightface buffalo\_sc}) with no
fallback for the small fraction of undetected frames ($4$ of $61{,}996$
on Replay; $0$ on 3DMAD). (iii) The 8\,GB GTX 1070 budget bounds
architecture and augmentation; a larger model or domain adaptation
might close the cross-dataset gap further.

\section{CSMAD Out-of-Distribution Probe}
\label{sec:csmad}

\subsection{Setup}

CSMAD (Custom Silicone Mask Attack Database) ships HDF5 captures from
an Intel RealSense SR300 (RGB, NIR, depth) and a Seek thermal camera,
with $87$ bonafide videos and $159$ attack videos in two pose families
(\texttt{WEAR} and \texttt{STAND}); we use only the SR300
\texttt{color} channel. Each frame is a separate timestamped dataset
of shape $(H,W,3)$ \texttt{uint8} under \texttt{data/sr300/color}.
The extractor lists keys, sorts them chronologically, and steps
through with \texttt{FRAME\_STRIDE=5}, exactly as for the other
datasets. The extractor handles two naming conventions across
WEAR/STAND subfolders:
\texttt{\{camera\}\_atk\_}\allowbreak\texttt{\{wearer\}\{mask\}\_...h5}
(WEAR) and
\texttt{Mask\_atk\_}\allowbreak\texttt{\{wearer\}\{mask\}\_...h5} (STAND).
After extraction, CSMAD contributes $18{,}763$ frames across $243$
videos.

CSMAD is held out entirely from training in every model reported in
this paper. The attack archive only covers subjects A--F; we
distribute those six subjects across all three partitions so attacks
appear in every split, while the eleven distinct subjects supply

The most informative comparison is across the three rows of
Table~\ref{tab:cross-csmad}. The replay-only and combined models
behave nearly identically on CSMAD (HTER $\approx 0{.}46$--$0{.}48$,
AUC $\approx 0{.}63$): both reject almost every bonafide CSMAD face
at the default threshold (BPCER $\approx 0{.}92$--$0{.}94$) while
correctly accepting every silicone-mask attack as ``not a digital
replay'' (APCER $=0$). Devel calibration raises the threshold to
$\sim$$0{.}88$--$0{.}92$, which mostly trades BPCER for APCER without
changing HTER materially. The combined model does not inherit any
benefit from being trained on a wider data mix: its CSMAD score
distribution is dominated by the replay-attack feature direction it
learned, and that direction is largely orthogonal to silicone-mask
versus real-face.

The 3DMAD-only model is qualitatively different. It transfers to
CSMAD substantially better (HTER $0{.}23$, AUC $0{.}85$) than either
of the other two, and its error decomposition is symmetric (APCER
$0{.}52$, BPCER $0{.}08$ at threshold $0{.}5$, balancing to APCER
$0{.}25$ / BPCER $0{.}21$ at the devel-calibrated threshold). Both
3DMAD and CSMAD are rigid mask attacks recorded in controlled
laboratory conditions, and the structural cues a 3D-mask classifier
learns --- skin/material reflectance discontinuities, mask-edge
geometry, lack of fine micro-texture --- transfer to silicone masks
with most of their discriminative power intact. The much larger
sensor change (Kinect $\rightarrow$ SR300) does shift the operating
point: the calibrated threshold drops to $0{.}36$ where it sat near
$0{.}83$ within domain, indicating systematic score deflation, but
the ranking is preserved.

\textbf{Implications.} The combined model does not automatically
inherit the better of the two single-dataset transfers --- on CSMAD it
inherits the worse one --- which pushes back on the naive ``more
diverse data is always better'' framing: when one source is structurally
useful for a new target and the other is orthogonal, mixing dilutes the
useful prior. Failure mode here is calibration, not ranking: all three
AUCs exceed $0{.}5$ ($0{.}63$--$0{.}85$), so sensor shift hurts the
operating point more than the decision direction.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented a unified RGB preprocessing pipeline that collapses
Replay-Attack and 3DMAD into a single manifest of $77{,}296$ uniformly
cropped $256{\times}256$ face images, and trained AttackNet v2.2 --- a
compact $8{.}4$M-parameter residual CNN --- from scratch on their
union. Within-dataset test ACER is below $2\%$ on both benchmarks, and
cross-dataset evaluation reveals a sharp asymmetry: Replay $\rightarrow$
3DMAD transfers at $2{.}8\%$ frame ACER (HTER $8{.}8\%$), while 3DMAD
$\rightarrow$ Replay collapses to $38{.}8\%$ (HTER $22{.}7\%$).

A devel-calibrated probe on the held-out CSMAD corpus produces the
sharpest finding: the 3DMAD-only model transfers to CSMAD substantially
better (HTER $0{.}23$, AUC $0{.}85$) than either the combined or the
replay-only model (HTER $\approx 0{.}46$--$0{.}48$, AUC $\approx 0{.}63$).
Combined training does not automatically inherit the better single-source
prior; for silicone-mask threats, structural similarity between training
and target attack types is the variable that survives a sensor change,
and that signal matters more than dataset size.
